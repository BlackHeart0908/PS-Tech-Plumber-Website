Whether you're in a standalone bungalow in Bhawarkuan, a new apartment in Nipania, or an older home in Mahalaxmi Nagar, having the right overhead water tank is the foundation of reliable water supply in Indore. Get the sizing or material wrong and you'll face repeated repairs, water shortage, or algae-contaminated water.

## Step 1: Calculate the Right Capacity

The standard rule: **150–200 litres per person per day** for an Indore household that includes cooking, bathing, toilet flushing, and basic cleaning.

| Household size | Minimum tank capacity |
|---|---|
| 1–2 people | 500–750 litres |
| 3–4 people | 1,000–1,500 litres |
| 5–6 people | 2,000 litres |
| Society/multi-floor | 5,000–10,000 litres |

For Indore homes that depend on municipal supply (which can be irregular — 2–3 hours per day in some areas), size up by 30–50% to have a buffer.

## Step 2: Choose the Right Material

### HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene) — Recommended
The standard for Indore homes. Brands like **Sintex**, **Penguin**, and **Vectus** make triple-layer HDPE tanks with a carbon black outer layer (blocks UV and prevents algae), a white foam middle layer for insulation, and a food-grade inner layer. These last 15–20 years with basic maintenance.

### Stainless Steel
More expensive but excellent for hard-water areas (no corrosion, easy to clean). Worth considering for Vijay Nagar and Nipania where hard borewell water can stain HDPE tanks over time.

### Old Concrete/Ferro-Cement
Many older Indore homes have concrete tanks built into the roof structure. These need to be inspected regularly for cracks and lined internally. If yours is more than 20 years old and shows hairline cracks, consider replacing or lining it — bacterial contamination in cracked tanks is a real risk.

**Avoid:** Very cheap single-layer plastic tanks. They become brittle in Indore's extreme summer heat (42–45°C) and develop micro-cracks within 3–5 years.

## Step 3: Plan the Inlet and Outlet Fittings Properly

This is where most DIY tank installations go wrong:

- **Inlet (float valve):** A quality brass float valve (ball cock) that shuts off flow when the tank is full. Cheap plastic float valves fail in Indore's hard water within a year. We recommend ISI-marked brass valves.
- **Outlet:** At least 5–8 cm from the bottom of the tank, so sediment doesn't flow into your household supply.
- **Overflow:** Sized at least as large as the inlet pipe, directed away from the wall so you notice when the tank is overflowing rather than having water silently damage the roof.
- **Drain valve:** At the lowest point, so the tank can be emptied for annual cleaning.
- **Mesh over all openings:** Indore has year-round insects. All tank vents should have fine stainless steel mesh to prevent mosquito breeding — especially important during monsoon.

## Step 4: Tank Placement and Structural Load

A 1,000-litre full tank weighs approximately 1,000 kg. Ensure your roof slab or tank platform can take this load — especially critical in Indore's older constructions. A structural check is advisable before installing any tank larger than 1,500 litres on a roof not originally designed for it.

For added pressure, position the tank as high as possible — every metre of height adds ~0.1 bar of water pressure. Indore homes often have 1–2 metres of head, which is adequate for ground and first floors but may feel low on upper floors of a 3-storey house.

## Step 5: Annual Maintenance

Clean your water tank every 6–12 months:
1. Empty completely through the drain valve
2. Scrub inside with a soft brush and dilute chlorine solution
3. Rinse thoroughly
4. Check and replace float valve if sticking
