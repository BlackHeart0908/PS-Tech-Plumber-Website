It's 6 AM in January and your geyser isn't delivering hot water. Cold showers in Indore winters are nobody's idea of fun. Before you call a plumber, run through this checklist — in our experience, about 30% of "geyser not working" calls turn out to be simple fixes homeowners can do themselves.

## Step 1: Check the MCB (Miniature Circuit Breaker)

Geysers draw 2–3 kW of power. A voltage fluctuation or momentary overload can trip the MCB for that circuit.

**What to do:** Go to your switchboard and look for a tripped MCB (it'll be in the middle position, neither fully up nor fully down). Reset it by pushing it fully off, then back on. Turn the geyser on and wait 15–20 minutes.

If it trips again, stop — there's an electrical fault, not just a geyser issue. Call an electrician or plumber.

## Step 2: Check the Geyser Power Indicator

Most geysers have a red (heating) and green (ready) indicator. If neither light is on:
- Check the wall socket with another appliance — the socket may be dead.
- Check the geyser's own power switch on the front panel.
- Check if the ISI-rated power cord is fully inserted.

## Step 3: Check the Thermostat Setting

Geyser thermostats can be accidentally turned down (especially the dial types on older Bajaj or Racold geysers). The thermostat is usually accessible under a small panel on the back of the unit.

**Normal setting:** 55–65°C for typical use. In Indore's winters, you may want 60–65°C. Above 70°C can cause scaling buildup faster.

## Step 4: Check Your Incoming Cold Water Supply

A storage geyser needs cold water flowing in to push hot water out. If your overhead tank is empty or the inlet valve is closed, you won't get hot water even if the element is working fine.

**What to do:** Open a cold tap elsewhere in the house. If there's no water pressure there either, the issue is your supply, not the geyser.

## Step 5: Check the Pressure Relief Valve (PRV)

The PRV is a safety valve on the side of the geyser that opens if pressure builds too high. If it's dripping constantly, it may have opened to release overpressure and then stuck slightly open — causing the cold water inlet valve to keep refilling, making your "hot water" actually lukewarm.

**What to do:** Gently lift the PRV lever for 2 seconds and release it. If it keeps dripping after this, it needs replacement — a straightforward job for a plumber.

## Step 6: Listen and Smell

**Rumbling or popping sounds:** Sediment buildup at the bottom of the tank. Hard water in Indore causes heavy limescale that settles and gets superheated, causing these sounds. This sediment also insulates the element from the water, making heating less efficient.

**Burning smell:** The heating element may be dry-firing (no water in the tank) or there's an electrical fault. Switch off and call immediately.

**Rotten egg smell:** Sulphur bacteria in stagnant water. Flush the tank and consider raising the thermostat to 70°C temporarily to kill bacteria.

## Step 7: Try the Reset Button

Most modern geysers (Racold, Havells, AO Smith) have a thermal cutout reset button behind a small access hole at the back. If the element overheated, this safety cutout trips. Press it with a thin pen. If it keeps tripping, the element or thermostat needs replacement.
