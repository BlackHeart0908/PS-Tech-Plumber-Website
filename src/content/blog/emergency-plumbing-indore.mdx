A plumbing emergency rarely gives warning. You walk into the kitchen to find water pouring from under the sink. The bathroom fills in minutes from an overflowing toilet. A pipe bursts somewhere in the wall at 11 PM. The first 10 minutes are crucial — the actions you take right now determine how much damage you'll be dealing with.

## Step 1: Stop the Water at the Source

**For any indoor emergency** — your first move is shutting off the water supply.

- **Under-sink isolation valve:** Each kitchen and bathroom fixture has a small valve on the supply pipe under the basin. Turn it clockwise (right) to close. This stops water to just that one fixture.
- **Main inlet valve:** Usually located near your water meter, overhead tank, or at the point where the municipality supply enters your building. For a burst pipe or wall leak, turn off the main inlet. This stops water flowing anywhere in the house.
- **Geyser valve:** If the leak is from or near the geyser, close the cold water inlet valve to the geyser (typically a blue-handled valve on the cold inlet pipe). Also switch off the geyser's MCB.

Locate your main valve now — before an emergency — so you know exactly where it is.

## Step 2: Deal with the Overflow Immediately

**Overflowing toilet:** Remove the lid from the cistern and push the float ball down with your hand to stop refilling. Then close the isolation valve behind the toilet (turn clockwise). Don't flush again until the blockage is cleared.

**Burst pipe or fitting:** Once the main supply is off, the immediate flow stops. But there will still be water draining from the pipe. Grab buckets, towels, and mops. Place buckets under drips and block gaps with rolled towels to direct water away from electronics and floors.

**Flooded room:** If a significant amount of water has entered a room, your priority is removing it quickly to prevent it reaching electrical sockets. Use a mop, wet vacuum, or buckets. If water is near electrical outlets, do NOT enter until you have turned off the MCB for that room.

## Step 3: Protect Your Electrics

Water and electricity are a dangerous combination. If water has reached or is near electrical points:
- Switch off the MCBs for the affected rooms at your distribution board
- Do not use any electrical appliance in the flooded area
- Do not touch plug points or light switches with wet hands

If you're unsure whether the area is electrically safe, stay out and wait for the plumber to assess.

## Step 4: Document the Damage

Once immediate safety is handled, take photos and videos. Walk through the affected areas. This helps:
- The plumber diagnose the problem faster
- Your insurance claim (if applicable)
- Track which items were damaged

## Step 6: Don't Use Chemical Drain Unblockers in an Emergency

For clogged drains that are overflowing, the temptation is to pour drain unblocker (acid or alkali based) into the pipe. **Don't do this during a flooding emergency.** The chemical won't work fast enough, and if the plumber then needs to handle the pipe, they'll be exposed to the chemical. Manual or mechanical clearing is always safer and faster.

## Common Indore Plumbing Emergencies and Their Causes

**Burst pipe in the wall:** Often happens in winter when metal pipes contract, or in summer when plastic pipes soften from heat. Also common in old GI pipes that have been corroding for years.

**Toilet flooding:** Usually a blocked drain that wasn't cleared before it became an overflow. Also caused by a stuck open fill valve that keeps sending water from the cistern to the bowl.

**Geyser leaks:** Tank corrosion (rust), failed pressure relief valve, or cracked inlet/outlet pipe connection. Always switch off the MCB and inlet valve first.

**Drain backflow during monsoon:** Indore's monsoon season (July–August) can overwhelm older drain lines in Khajrana, Mahalaxmi Nagar, and other established colonies. A [sump pump installation](/blog/sump-pump-monsoon-indore) before the rains is the lasting solution.
