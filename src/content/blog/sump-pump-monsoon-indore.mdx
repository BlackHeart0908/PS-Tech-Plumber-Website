Indore receives 850–1,100 mm of rainfall concentrated in 3 months (June–August). For homes with basements, stilt parking, or low-lying plots, this means a familiar annual problem: water seeping in through walls and floor, flooding the basement parking or utility area by morning.

A sump pump is the reliable, permanent solution. Here's everything you need to know before installing one.

## What Is a Sump Pump?

A sump pump is a submersible electric pump installed in a small pit (the "sump") dug at the lowest point of your basement or flood-prone area. As groundwater rises and seeps into the pit through drain holes, the pump automatically activates via a float switch (like your toilet's fill valve) and pumps the water out through a discharge pipe to a safe drainage point outside the building.

Modern sump pumps in India are compact, quiet, and automatic — most homeowners set them up before the monsoon and don't need to think about them again.

## Does Your Indore Home Need a Sump Pump?

You probably need one if:
- Your basement or stilt parking floods during heavy rain
- Your ground floor is at or below road level (common in older Indore construction)
- You live in a low-lying area or near a nala (drainage channel) — particularly relevant in parts of Khajrana, Banganga, and some Bhawarkuan sub-areas
- You've experienced basement flooding at least once in the last 3 monsoons

You may not need one if:
- You live on upper floors of a well-drained society with a central sump system managed by the RWA
- Your ground level is significantly above the road

## Choosing the Right Sump Pump for Your Indore Home

### Step 1: Calculate the Required Capacity

Sump pump capacity is measured in litres per hour (LPH) or litres per minute (LPM).

For a typical 1–2 car basement parking area in Indore (approx. 20×10 m, 25 cm of potential flooding):
- Volume: 50,000 litres
- Required pump-out in heavy rain: within 1–2 hours
- Minimum pump capacity needed: ~500 LPM (30,000 LPH)

For a small basement utility area (4×4 m):
- Minimum: 200 LPM

**Buy slightly above your calculated minimum** — pump capacity drops when the discharge pipe rises (head pressure). A 50% buffer is sensible.

### Step 2: Choose a Reliable Brand

Indian sump pump brands worth considering:
- **Kirloskar** — the most widely available and serviced brand in central India. Reliable for Indian conditions.
- **Grundfos** — premium, energy efficient, longer life, higher cost
- **CRI Pumps** — good value, widely available in Madhya Pradesh
- **Oswal / Lucky** — budget options adequate for occasional monsoon use

Avoid unbranded Chinese pumps from local hardware stores — they typically fail within a season.

### Step 3: Automatic Float Switch

Always specify a pump with an **automatic float switch** (or install one separately). Without this, you need to manually turn the pump on when water rises and off when it drains — easy to miss during a night storm. An auto float switch handles this for you.

### Step 4: Discharge Pipe Routing

The discharge pipe must:
- Rise vertically from the pit, then run horizontally to an outdoor drain or soak pit
- Not discharge back toward the building foundation
- Have a check valve to prevent back-flow when the pump stops

## The Sump Pit Installation

The sump pit is typically 45–60 cm diameter and 60–90 cm deep, dug at the lowest corner of the basement. The sides are fitted with perforated concrete rings or a purpose-made plastic sump liner to allow groundwater to flow in while keeping sediment out.

4. Running the discharge pipe to an appropriate outlet

## Before and After Monsoon Maintenance

**Before monsoon (May–June):**
- Test the pump by pouring water into the pit
- Check the float switch activates correctly
- Clear the discharge pipe outlet of any leaves or debris

**After monsoon (October):**
- Run the pump dry briefly and clean the pit of any sediment
- Store properly if removing the pump (unlikely — most stay installed year-round)
